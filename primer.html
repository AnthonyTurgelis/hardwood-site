You know basketball. You don't need to know statistics. This walks you up from "what does the scoreboard usually look like" to "how do we grade our own predictions" — and at every step it shows you a real number the platform computes today and points you at the tab where you can see it live.

The whole thing rests on one honest idea: basketball is mostly signal wrapped in a lot of noise. Good analytics isn't about pretending the noise isn't there — it's about measuring how much is signal, how much is noise, and being honest about which is which. That honesty is the last section, and it's the most important one.

Section 1

The scoreboard, quantified

The concept Before you can say a team "scored a lot" or "played slow," you need a baseline — the normal shape of a WNBA game. Every number that follows is measured against this baseline.

Across every WNBA game from 2015 through 2026, two teams combine for about 162 points a night, roughly 81 each. The game isn't evenly paced: the first quarter is the highest-scoring (about 41.8 combined points), drifting down to about 39.5 by the fourth as legs tire and fouls pile up.
Measured across 2,600 games · Quarter Scoreline explorer

Why it matters — the number that runs everything is the noise. In any single quarter, the margin can swing by about 7.4 points of pure scoring randomness. That one figure is the hinge of this whole primer. It's why a 4-point lead early means almost nothing, why blowouts happen between evenly-matched teams, and why no model — ours or a Vegas book's — can ever predict a single game precisely. Basketball is a game where the noise is genuinely large relative to the skill gap on any given night.

You can watch that noise compound as the game goes on. The typical margin between two even teams grows from about ±7.6 points after one quarter to ±13.8 by the final buzzer — the scoreboard spreads apart not because one team is "taking over," but because random point-swings accumulate.

On the board This is the front door to the Deep Dives tab (the Quarter Scoreline explorer). It shows the shape of a game before any team names are entered — the league-average matchup you measure everything else against.

Section 2

What a lead is worth — and why leads aren't safe

The concept A lead is not a fixed amount of safety. The same lead is worth more the later you hold it, because there's less time left for the noise from Section 1 to erase it.

Start two evenly-matched teams on a neutral floor — a coin flip, 50/50 at tip. Now give one of them a 6-point lead:

Holding a 6-point lead…Wins about
After the first quarter66%
At halftime74%
After the third quarter76%

Same six points, very different security. A smaller edge tells the same story: up 3 barely matters early (59% after the first quarter) but is a real advantage late (66% after the third). Up 10 after the third quarter is close to safe at 92% — but notice it is not automatic.

The honest twist. A naive "random-walk" model says a 6-point lead entering the fourth should be worth about 86%. In reality it's only 76%. WNBA teams come back late more often than the clean math predicts — late fouling, three-point variance, and leaders coasting all give the trailing team fatter tails. So the platform's rule is: for late-game leads, trust the counted history, not the tidy formula.
Quarter Scoreline explorer

You can also see the game "decide itself" gradually: the share of games still within 6 points falls from 61% after the first quarter to 33% at the final buzzer.

On the board The Quarter Scoreline explorer lets you drop a marker on any lead at any quarter break and read the win probability — with the counted (empirical) line drawn solid and the on-paper model dashed, so the late-game comeback gap is visible, not hidden.

Section 3

Team rating — what "strength" really means

The concept Win-loss record is a bad way to rank teams — it hides how they won (by 20 or by 1) and who they played. Our rating instead measures a team in points: how many points better or worse than an average team they are, adjusted for opponent. A rating of +5 means "about five points better than league-average on a neutral floor."

Read that directly: on a neutral floor, Minnesota would be favored over an average team by about nine points, and over Atlanta (+5.2) by roughly the difference, about four.

Why it matters — two numbers, not one. A season-long rating is stable but slow. So the board also carries a recent-form (momentum) number — how a team has been trending lately, apart from its full-season body of work. Portland, for instance, shows a strongly positive momentum swing (about +2.2) even while its season rating is still low — a team playing better than its record. The two together answer "how good are they?" and "how good are they right now?" without letting a hot streak overwrite a whole season.

On the board On the Teams tab, each team shows its strength (the point rating), its recent-form swing, its playoff odds, and its title odds from a season simulation. Strength is the honest talent estimate; the momentum number is the "don't ignore the last two weeks" adjustment; the odds are what those translate to once you simulate the rest of the schedule thousands of times.

Section 4

Home court, rest, and the schedule

The concept Fans feel these edges — the home crowd, the tired road team on a back-to-back — but rarely quantify them. Analytics puts a point value on each.

Home court. Across the full history, home teams win 55.5% of the time and outscore visitors by about +2.1 points on average — the single most reliable "free" edge in the sport. Our prediction model bakes in a home-court adjustment of about +1.9 points, very close to the raw history, which is a good sign the model isn't inventing an edge the data doesn't support.
Quarter Scoreline explorer · home-court measurement

Practically: if two even teams meet and one is home, don't read them as 50/50 — read the home side as starting roughly two points and five percentage points ahead of the neutral numbers in Section 2.

Rest and back-to-backs. The platform already stores, for every player-game, how many days of rest a team had and whether it was the second night of a back-to-back. The exact point cost of a rest disadvantage is a deep-dive still on the build queue, so this primer does not quote a precise "a back-to-back costs X points" figure yet — that number isn't shipped, and inventing one would violate the honesty rule this whole primer runs on. What we can say today is that the raw material is in hand and the edge is real and measurable; the quantified answer lands when that dive ships.

On the board Home court shows up inside every game prediction (the favorite already has it baked in). The rest/schedule dive will live in the Deep Dives hub alongside the scoreline explorer when built.

Section 5

Player value beyond points

The concept The leading scorer is not always the most valuable player. Points are the loudest stat but not the most complete one — a player can help a team win through defense, rebounding, playmaking, and simply the fact that the team is better with them on the floor. Our impact rating measures exactly that: roughly how many points per game a team gains from a player being on the court, beyond what shows up in their scoring line.

A'ja Wilson leads the players board with 25.6 points a game and a +1.2 on-court impact rating — she scores the most and moves the needle too, which is rare. More instructive is the "impact riser": Sika Kone of Atlanta ranks 32nd in the league by impact but only 169th by scoring — a gap of 137 spots. She scores about 2 points a game and would be invisible on a scoring list, yet her team is measurably better with her out there. That gap — valuable but quiet — is the entire reason impact rating exists.
Players board

Minutes matter too. Value only counts if a player is on the floor, so the board projects expected minutes for every player (Wilson projects around 31). Those projections are graded: we miss a player's actual minutes by about 6 minutes per game and their points by about 3.9 points per game on average. That's honest and useful — it tells you how much to trust a single-game projection.

On the board On the Players tab, each player shows scoring, the on-court impact rating alongside it, share of team minutes, projected minutes, and a plausible range of box-score outcomes. Scoring tells you the volume, impact tells you whether the team actually wins more with them out there, and the two disagreeing (like Sika Kone) is a signal, not an error.

Section 6

The currency of a roster — wins and dollars

The concept To compare a center to a guard, or a star's salary to her production, you need a common currency. Ours has three convertible faces: on-court rating, Wins Above Replacement (WAR) — how many wins a player adds over a freely-available replacement — and dollars-per-win, the price a team pays per win. The Coach dashboard reads this tactically; the GM dashboard reads it as an asset ledger.

Wins, not points — Wilson is worth roughly nine-and-a-half wins more than a replacement-level player.

The dollar layer is where it gets sharp. The league pays a median of about $405,000 per win. Against that, rookie-scale stars are staggering bargains: Paige Bueckers projects to about 5.8 wins at roughly $86,000 per win — a quarter of the going rate — while a veteran like Alyssa Thomas costs about $203,000 per win. Same currency, and it instantly tells a GM who is underpaid and who is a luxury.
Value spine · Coach and GM dashboards

Coverage, stated honestly. We carry 208 players; 141 have a wins-added projection and 119 have a 2026 salary attached — so the ledger is broad but not complete, and a player with no bar simply isn't projected yet rather than being worth zero. Players are also tagged with one of five skill archetypes (Scoring Anchor, Two-Way Creator, Floor General, Defensive Anchor, Balanced) so the Coach view can reason about roster fit, not just totals.

On the board The Coach and GM tabs both read this one shared spine. Coach uses it to find a matchup's difference-makers; GM uses the dollars-per-win column to spot value and the trade board to move it.

Section 7

Uncertainty is the point — ranges, not just guesses

The concept A prediction that says "home team by 7" and nothing else is hiding the most important part: how sure are we? Every honest projection carries a range, and the range is calibrated — meaning when we say "90% likely to land in this band," the result should actually land there about 90% of the time.

The platform's proudest honest number. When our model draws a 90% margin range, the real result falls inside it 90.4% of the time; our 80% range catches it 80.8%. Those are as close to perfectly-calibrated as it gets — a 90% band that catches 90.4% is telling the truth about its own confidence. A model whose "90% range" only caught 70% of results would be lying to you; ours isn't.
Calibration check · 167 graded games

Why it matters. A calibrated range is what lets you use a prediction responsibly. "Home by 7, and 90% confident the margin lands between −4 and +18" is an honest statement you can act on. "Home by 7" alone is false precision. The wide ranges aren't a weakness — they're the model correctly telling you a single WNBA game is genuinely hard to pin down (Section 1's noise, quantified into a band).

On the board The accuracy tile on the home surface shows these coverage numbers live. When you read a game prediction, the band around the margin is the part to respect — the point estimate is the middle of a real distribution, not a promise.

Section 8

How good is the board, honestly?

The concept The last and most important habit: grade yourself in public. We keep a game-by-game scorecard and publish the miss instead of hiding it.

10.9
points — average margin miss (163 graded games)
64.4%
right winner called

A longer season-reconstruction read puts the average margin miss at about 10.8 points, and among games where we named a clear favorite, that favorite actually won about 68% of the time. There is no systematic lean — over the season our calls sit within a point of dead-center, so we don't quietly favor home or road teams.

Is ~11 points good? Yes — it's close to the practical floor. The misses that hurt are blowouts: games that turn into runaways nobody saw coming. Past about eleven points, the result is mostly shooting luck that no model — or betting market — can predict. We're field-standard for a model built on public data, and we say so rather than dressing it up.

What we honestly cannot do yet

Being a real analytics platform means naming the gaps, not just the wins. Today we cannot:

  • Five-player lineup on/off splits — which exact five-woman units outscore opponents. We have team ratings and per-player impact, but not clean stint-level lineup data. A "best lineups" feature is a data-collection project first, not something buildable now.
  • Player tracking — speed, distance, drives, contested shots, defensive matchups. The optical-tracking feed is confirmed dead for the WNBA (verified empty across every season and host we probed). Shot location we have (from play-by-play coordinates); shot contest quality and defender matchups we do not.
  • Play-type tagging — pick-and-roll, isolation, post-up efficiency. That labeling isn't in our data.
  • Referee-level data — no crew-assignment feed, so "which officials call it tighter" is unsupported.
  • Deep fatigue / travel load — we have rest-days and back-to-back flags, but not travel miles or rolling minutes-load, so fatigue claims stay caveated.

None of these are hidden behind a vague "coming soon." They're the honest edge of what public WNBA data supports today — and naming them is exactly the discipline that makes the numbers in Sections 1–7 trustworthy.

In one paragraph

The whole primer, distilled

A WNBA game is about 162 points and roughly 7 points of pure quarter-to-quarter noise, which is why early leads are fragile and even teams produce blowouts. A lead is worth more the later you hold it (up 6 climbs from 66% to 76% across the game), but late leads are less safe than the tidy math says. Teams are rated in points-above-average, not records; home court is worth about +2.1 points; players are measured by on-court impact and wins added, not just scoring, and priced in dollars-per-win. Every projection is a calibrated range — our 90% band really does catch 90% of results — and we grade ourselves in public: about an 11-point average margin miss, right winner ~64% of the time, with the gaps we can't yet fill named out loud.
